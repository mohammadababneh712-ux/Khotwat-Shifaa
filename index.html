Overview

Diabetes refers to a group of diseases that affect how the body uses blood sugar (glucose). Glucose is a vital source of energy for the cells that make up muscles and tissues. It is also the brain's main source of fuel.

Chronic diabetes conditions include Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. Potentially reversible diabetes conditions include prediabetes and gestational diabetes.

Symptoms

Symptoms depend on how high your blood sugar is. Some people may not have symptoms. In Type 1, symptoms tend to come on quickly.

  • Increased thirst and frequent urination.
  • Unintended weight loss.
  • Presence of ketones in the urine.
  • Fatigue and weakness.
  • Blurred vision and slow-healing sores.
  • Frequent infections (gums, skin, etc.).

Complications

Long-term complications develop gradually. They can include:

  • Cardiovascular disease: Increased risk of heart attack and stroke.
  • Nerve & Kidney damage: Affecting feet and filtration systems.
  • Eye damage: Can lead to blindness.
  • Skin conditions: Susceptibility to infections.
  • Alzheimer's risk.

Prevention

  • Eat Healthy: Choose foods lower in fat and calories and higher in fiber.
  • Get Physical: Aim for about 30 minutes of moderate aerobic activity daily.
  • Lose Excess Weight: Losing 7% of your body weight can reduce the risk.

Overview

Cancer refers to a large number of diseases characterized by the development of abnormal cells that divide uncontrollably. It is the second leading cause of death globally, though survival rates are improving.

Symptoms

Signs vary by body part, but general symptoms include:

  • Fatigue or lump/thickening felt under the skin.
  • Weight changes (unintended loss or gain).
  • Skin changes (yellowing, darkening, or redness).
  • Changes in bowel or bladder habits.
  • Persistent cough, trouble breathing, or difficulty swallowing.
  • Unexplained joint pain, fevers, or night sweats.

Causes & Risk Factors

Cancer is caused by mutations in DNA. Risk factors include:

  • Age: Most common in people 65 years or older.
  • Habits: Smoking, excessive alcohol, and unsafe sun exposure.
  • Family History: Inherited genetic mutations.
  • Environment: Exposure to harmful chemicals (like asbestos) or passive smoking.

Prevention

  • Stop Smoking: This reduces risk for many types of cancer.
  • Sun Safety: Avoid excessive sun and use sunscreen.
  • Healthy Diet: Eat plenty of fruits and vegetables; limit processed meats.
  • Exercise: Aim for 30 minutes of activity most days.
  • Vaccinations: Hepatitis B and HPV vaccines can prevent certain cancers.
